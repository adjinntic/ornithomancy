My esteemed colleague,

Your inquiry regarding Hartford's placement in the greater pattern has led me down some rather curious paths of investigation. I write to you now from the private reading room of the Athenaeum, where certain convergences have become... apparent.

Consider first the ravens of Mark Twain's house - did you know he kept them? Yes, the great satirist harbored these messengers of Odin in his curious Eastern-inspired mansion. The very same species that Hartford now hosts in abundance near the Ancient Burying Ground. (You'll recall, of course, that Poe himself lectured here in 1849, bringing his own dark bird's influence). But it's not merely the corvids that draw my attention.

The true significance, I believe, lies in Hartford's position along the great river. The Connecticut - whose name derives from the Algonquian "quinetucket," meaning "beside the long tidal river" - carries more than mere commerce. The Dutch traders who first established here spoke of strange lights upon the water at night, reminiscent of the foxfire phenomena described in certain Rosicrucian texts.

But here is something that may particularly interest you: In 1887, a curious group of transcendentalists established what they called the "Hartford Circle for Philosophical Research." Their meeting place? A stone's throw from where Samuel Colt would later build his famous blue onion dome - itself an architectural anomaly that seems to echo certain Hermetic principles of sacred geometry. The dome, I'm told, aligns perfectly with the winter solstice sunrise when viewed from the old Dutch point.

Most intriguing of all - and I hesitate to commit this to paper - is the matter of Wallace Stevens. The insurance executive poet walks precisely the same route to work each day, composing verses in his head. His path, when mapped, bears striking similarity to certain sigils found in the marginalia of Cotton Mather's private papers (which, as you know, found their way to Hartford's Trinity College library through channels best left unexplored).

I've enclosed a curious diagram found in the Athenaeum's restricted collection, showing the intersection points of:

  • The old Native American trading paths
  • The locations of the city's earliest churches
  • The flight patterns of local birds during the autumn migration

When overlaid with the modern street grid, it forms an unmistakable pattern that recalls the geometric studies of John Dee.

One final note - the recent construction of the Travelers Tower seems to have altered something in the local aetheric currents. The insurance men claim it's merely an office building, but its height (527 feet) corresponds too precisely with certain measurements found in the Picatrix to be mere coincidence.

I trust you will treat this information with appropriate discretion. Some of our colleagues in the Society might find these connections... unsettling.

With regards to your upcoming visitor - might I suggest directing their attention to the morning gatherings of birds at Bushnell Park? The hour just before sunrise proves most illuminating, particularly near the ancient elm that survived the great flood of 1936 (though I realize I'm getting ahead of our present time).

Yours in the Great Work,

Personal Journal Entry: On Receipt of the Ibis Seal

Found among the private papers of Dr. Reed
December 21, 1923
Time: Just past midnight

I must record this while my hands still steady enough to hold the pen. After twenty years of research into pattern transmission, after countless dead ends in pursuing the ME's paradoxical nature, after all those whispered references to the Maison that seemed to evaporate upon closer inspection - it has found me.

The knock came at precisely the moment I was examining the curious crystalline formations in tablet K-4891. Three sharp raps, then silence. No footsteps retreating.

When I opened my door, the hallway was empty save for a perfect black cube, perhaps six inches on each side, its surface possessing that peculiar quality of both absorbing and reflecting light that I've only seen once before, in certain artifacts from the [redacted] collection. Atop it lay an envelope of heavy black paper, sealed with what I first took to be lapis lazuli but now suspect to be something far more curious - the wax seems to shift its patterning when viewed from different angles, though the Ibis figure remains constant.

The invitation inside (which even now seems to resist my attempts to recall its exact wording) speaks of a gathering. But more than that - it speaks of exactly those patterns I've spent my life pursuing. The text itself seems to operate on multiple levels, each reading revealing new layers of meaning. Only the signature remains constant: a simple "-S" in a hand that somehow manages to suggest both ancient calligraphy and thoroughly modern precision.

The cube remains unopened. I've tried every conventional approach, but its method of opening (if indeed it is meant to be opened) eludes me. When I hold it, I'm reminded of that passage from the Emerald Tablet: "That which is below is like that which is above..." The cube seems to embody this principle - each face reflecting the others in ways that defy conventional geometry.

Most telling is what the invitation does not say. No specific time is mentioned, yet I feel with inexplicable certainty that I will know when to attend. No location is given, yet something in the pattern of the text suggests this too will become clear.

I find myself wondering - have I been pursuing the Maison all these years, or has it been waiting for the right moment to reveal itself? The references to the Ibis House in those fragmentary Hermetic texts take on new meaning now. How many others have received such invitations throughout history? What transforms within that mysterious gathering?

The cube pulses with a subtle warmth as I write this, as if responding to my questions with its own. I'm reminded of Enki's gift to Inanna - how many of the ME were simultaneously object and concept, pattern and manifestation?

The morning birds have begun their songs. The cube's surface has changed somehow during the night. Or perhaps it is I who have changed in looking at it.

-E.W.R.

PREFATORY NOTES ON THE ME AND THE REED MANUSCRIPT

The document that follows represents one of the more intriguing scholarly approaches to what ancient Sumerians called the "ME" (pronounced "may"). For modern readers unfamiliar with this fundamental concept of ancient Mesopotamian civilization, some context is essential.

The ME were a collection of divine powers, attributes, or archetypal patterns that governed the fabric of civilization itself. Think of them as the source code of reality - but that analogy, while useful, barely scratches the surface of their complexity. They were simultaneously abstract concepts, physical objects, and transferable powers.

The primary narrative concerning the ME comes from "Inanna and Enki," one of humanity's oldest recorded stories. In it, Inanna, the ambitious goddess of love and war, visits Enki, the god of wisdom and fresh water, in his watery domain of Eridu. During a feast, Enki, influenced by beer (a divine power itself included in the ME), gifts Inanna over 100 ME. These include such diverse concepts as:

  • Godship
  • Truth
  • Descent into the underworld
  • The art of lovemaking
  • The craft of the woodworker
  • Power
  • Deceit
  • The measuring of time
  • The making of decisions
  • Musical instruments
  • Warmth
  • Various priestly offices
  • The destruction of cities
  • Victory

When Enki sobers up and realizes what he's done, he sends demons to recover the ME, but Inanna has already escaped to her city of Uruk, essentially transferring civilization's fundamental powers from Eridu to her domain.

What makes this story and the ME themselves so fascinating to scholars like Dr. Reed is their dual nature - they are simultaneously conceptual and tangible. The texts describe them being physically loaded onto Inanna's boat, yet they include abstract concepts like "hero-ship" and "the making of judgments." This paradox has puzzled scholars for generations.

Dr. Reed's manuscript, found among her effects after her unexplained departure from academia in 1923, represents one of the more unorthodox approaches to understanding the ME. While her conclusions may seem radical to some, her work provides an interesting framework for considering how ancient peoples understood the relationship between physical objects and abstract powers.

The water damage to the original manuscript has led to much speculation. Some suggest it was deliberately exposed to water, given the connection to Enki's watery abode. Others point to more mundane explanations involving a burst pipe in the university archives. What remains undisputed is the document's influence on subsequent scholarship regarding pattern transmission across historical epochs.
